SLIDE 4 — WHO WE ARE / WHAT WE DO
================================================================================

WHO WE ARE:
The Sustainable Solutions Lab or SSL (ess-el) at UMass Boston is an applied
research and action institute working at the intersection of climate and equity.
We are a groundbreaking, cross-disciplinary partnership of five schools and four
institutes across the University:

  - Collaborative of Asian American, Native American, Latino, and African
    American Institutes (CANALA)
  - College of Liberal Arts
  - College of Management
  - College of Nursing and Health Sciences
  - John W. McCormack Graduate School of Policy and Global Studies
  - School for the Environment

WHAT WE DO:
Boston has the greatest racial wealth inequality in the U.S. and is the 8th most
vulnerable city to sea-level rise globally.

As part of Boston's only public research university, SSL's mission is to:
  1) Understand the disproportionate impacts of climate change on marginalized
     communities
  2) Partner with them to develop equitable and sustainable solutions.

SLIDE 5 — CLIMATE ACTION TERMINOLOGY
================================================================================

The first of UMass Boston's Campus Goals for the academic year 2020-21 is to
make UMass Boston a leading anti-racist, health promoting public research
university with excellence at its core. SSL's core values align with this goal.
The below definitions are key to understanding our work:

CLIMATE CHANGE
  A shift in average global weather patterns due to increased greenhouse gas
  emissions in the atmosphere. It disproportionately impacts low-income
  communities and communities of color.

SYSTEMIC RACISM
  Among other injustices, systemic racism causes these communities to suffer
  more pollution, failing infrastructure, and increased exposure to climate risks.

CLIMATE ADAPTATION
  Focused on better protecting people, homes and communities from the impacts
  of the climate crisis.

CLIMATE JUSTICE
  Climate action that recognizes past injustices and moves us closer to a world
  where everyone can thrive despite a changing climate.

SLIDES 11-12 — VIEWS THAT MATTER: RACE AND OPINIONS ON CLIMATE CHANGE
OF BOSTON AREA RESIDENTS
================================================================================

A growing body of research finds that climate change places a disproportionate
burden on communities of color in the United States. In spite of this evidence,
important discussions taking place about climate change — such as how it is
experienced, which policy solutions are most critical and how we can prepare for
and adapt to its impacts — often leave out the voices of the very communities
most affected.

The Views that Matter report attempts to address that gap by surveying the views
of climate change across racial groups in the Boston area and intentionally
including a large sampling of people of color. The report is released in
partnership with the Barr Foundation.

HEAR FROM THE AUTHORS:

"I think it is incredibly important for our voices to be in the table. Most of
the local and national studies on climate change done to date have only been
including the voices and the experiences of the white population."
— Lorna Rivera, Mauricio Gaston Institute for Latino Community

"We hear a lot of speculation about what communities of color might or might not
think about climate change, but we thought it was important to actually ask them."
— Rosalyn Negron, Associate Professor of Anthropology

"We need data that reflects everyone in society, especially significant and
long-silenced communities, if we want equitable climate change policies and
responses."
— J. Cedric Woods, Institute for New England Native American Studies

KEY FINDINGS:
  - Residents of color are more likely than white residents to believe that
    climate change is happening in Massachusetts (8 in 10) and is a very
    serious threat.
  - White respondents are the least inclined to view climate change impacts as
    affecting some people more than others.
  - Respondents, regardless of race, overwhelmingly agree that combatting
    climate change requires "major" steps at the federal and state levels.
  - Respondents of color are more inclined than whites to believe that the
    effects are shared "equally."
  - By a margin of about 2 to 1, respondents of all groups believe that climate
    change "can be stopped" as opposed to believing "it is too late."
  - Those living in high-resource areas and low-income neighborhoods differ in
    their readiness for climate impacts. High-resource neighborhoods (mostly
    white) are more likely to see themselves as "very prepared."

SLIDES 16-17 — STACY VANDEVEER
================================================================================

LEARNING FROM THE MASSACHUSETTS MUNICIPAL VULNERABILITY PREPAREDNESS (MVP)
PROGRAM IN THE GREATER BOSTON REGION

Research Team: Stacy D. VanDeveer (principal investigator), Patricia Bailey,
David Sulewski

Findings from the Basic Municipal Vulnerability Assessment for Climate Change
Adaptation in the Greater Boston Metropolitan Area. The objective of this study
is to understand conditions in and across municipalities. In 2020, over 80% of
Massachusetts cities and towns had participated in the MVP program and the
findings are being used to improve program design for equitable inclusion.

CONDITIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS FOR EQUITABLE CLIMATE RESILIENCE IN EAST BOSTON

Research examining environmental justice concerns and generating a better
understanding of how these apply in the context of climate planning for residents
through a justice lens. Using a framework of just adaptation, the research used
secondary document analysis about existing resilience plans, with recommendations
developed through partnerships with residents and organizations.

SLIDE 24 — CLIMATE JUSTICE RESEARCH DATABASE
================================================================================

Many SSL faculty are relatively new to the field of climate justice. To help them
ground their own scholarship in the academic conversation, Rosalyn Negron and
graduate student Katsyris Rivera-Kientz have created a database of climate justice
research articles. All research starts with a literature review. Launched in March
2021, this resource (housed in Mendeley) contains over 500 articles in a wide
variety of topics from health to housing to governance. All SSL faculty have
access to it and are able to contribute to it themselves so that it will continue
to evolve as expertise on campus grows. This is a central, living resource for SSL
faculty to begin their exploration of climate justice topics.

"Having access to the climate justice research database has allowed me to very
quickly get up to speed on where the academic conversations are in fields beyond
my own. It's giving me a broader interdisciplinary perspective that is essential
for the directions I want to take my research."
— Antonio Raciti, Assistant Professor of Ecological Design, School for the Environment

SLIDE 25 — RESEARCH GRANT WINNER
================================================================================

SSL CLIMATE JUSTICE RESEARCH GRANTS FOR FACULTY
In 2021, SSL awarded two research grants to support faculty conducting research
focused on climate justice. These grants, for up to $50,000, were open to
interdisciplinary teams of tenure or non-tenure track faculty members affiliated
with the colleges, schools and institutes that make up SSL.

PROJECT: Novel Research in Climate Justice: Including Transient Populations
in Climate Resiliency

School for Environment (SFE) associate dean Ellen Douglas, assistant professor
Evan Stewart from the College of Liberal Arts and SFE PhD candidate Johnna
Flahive are using their $48,000 Research Grant for a project that will
characterize the current status and vulnerability of transient populations
(homeless citizens and seasonal international visa workers) within the context
of climate-related risks, focusing on Boston and Cape Cod, Massachusetts. There
are many climate resiliency efforts underway in Massachusetts; however, all
efforts to date have seemingly overlooked transient populations. Their aim is to
identify historical barriers to and future opportunities for involving transient
populations in climate resilience planning and policies.

  Ellen Douglas — Associate Dean, Professor of Hydrology, School for the Environment
  Johnna Flahive — PhD Candidate, School for the Environment
  Evan Stewart — Assistant Professor of Sociology, College of Liberal Arts

SLIDE 26 — RESEARCH GRANT WINNER
================================================================================

PROJECT: Climate Justice, Traditional Ecological Knowledge, and Relationality
among Indigenous Peoples of New England and Quintana Roo, Mexico

A grant for $40,000 was awarded to an interdisciplinary study of traditional
ecological knowledge and climate change adaptations among the Indigenous peoples
of New England and Quintana Roo, Mexico. This project examines how Indigenous
peoples have been among the first to develop knowledge and adaptations based on
their relations and observations about the changes in the climate and its effect
on their environment. Although there is an increase in interdisciplinary climate
studies of Indigenous peoples and traditional ecological knowledge (TEK), there
are few studies in New England and the Maya region of Quintana Roo. This study
will expand the anthropological perspective to document the complexity of climate
change in discourse and practice in these regions.

  J. Cedric Woods — Director, Institute for New England Native American Studies
  Eduardo Siqueira — Associate Professor, College of Public and Community Service
  Jose Martinez-Reyes — Assistant Professor of Africana Studies, College of Liberal Arts
  Camille Curtis Martinez — Lecturer in Communication, College of Liberal Arts

SLIDE 27 — SEED GRANT WINNER
================================================================================

SSL CLIMATE JUSTICE SEED GRANTS FOR FACULTY
In 2020, SSL launched a Seed Grant Program to incentivize interdisciplinary
climate justice research. This initiative is part of a broader goal to foster
scholarship that examines the impact of climate change on those most vulnerable
to systemic inequities. Each Seed Grant award is for $7,500.

PROJECT: Leave the Trees: Culture, Climate Justice, and Black Diasporas in Boston

Professor Quito Swan and assistant professor Layla D. Brown-Vincent of the
Africana Studies department in the College of Liberal Arts are using their SSL
Seed Grant to explore how the Black Diaspora has engaged in climate justice
through a pop culture lens. The aim of the "Leave the Trees" project is to
generate an archive of discourse and experience that can support the development
of sustainable solutions for the Black community. Additionally, they will map
contemporary Black climate justice activists and organizations in the Boston area.

  Quito Swan — Professor of Africana Studies, College of Liberal Arts

SLIDE 28 — SEED GRANT WINNER
================================================================================

PROJECT: Climate Justice and the Power of Knowledge in Gloucester's Fishing
Industry

Over the last fifty years, federal and state policies driven by environmental
factors have profoundly affected the fishing industry of Gloucester,
Massachusetts. Assistant professors Antonio Raciti and Alan Wiig and associate
professor Rosalyn Negron of the School for the Environment are applying their SSL
Seed Grant funds to a project that integrates three disciplines (anthropology,
geography and planning) to examine how climate justice is perceived by the fishing
industry and the community. The research team is evaluating the impact of
stakeholders in the fishing industry having different understandings of what
climate justice means. The main goal is to identify a path that takes those varied
climate justice philosophies and transforms them into a shared vision of what
equitable decision-making looks like in the face of climate change.

  Antonio Raciti — Assistant Professor of Ecological Design, School for the Environment
  Alan Wiig — Assistant Professor, School for the Environment
  Rosalyn Negron — Associate Professor of Anthropology, College of Liberal Arts

SLIDE 29 — SEED GRANT WINNER
================================================================================

PROJECT: Hurricanes, Floods, and Fires: What are the Employment Impacts
of Climate Change?

Keren M. Horn, associate professor of economics, and Dania V. Francis, assistant
professor of economics in the College of Liberal Arts, are exploring the
employment impacts of natural hazards, particularly for lower-income and minority
individuals. Their work will examine how natural hazards, which are becoming
increasingly more likely, affect individual employment trajectories. Horn and
Francis are particularly interested in studying the impacts on those most
vulnerable to the negative consequences of climate change, specifically households
that have weaker connections to the labor market and fewer personal resources to
cushion them from the negative impacts of such a disaster. They hope to inform
federal policy responses to natural disasters in a way that will lead to more
equitable economic recoveries.

"Being awarded a SSL Seed Grant is allowing me to explore a project related to
climate change that I wouldn't have otherwise. As a housing and urban economist,
my research expertise is focused on issues of racial and economic justice but
connecting with SSL is opening up a new line of inquiry for me."
— Keren Horn

  Keren M. Horn — Associate Professor of Economics, College of Liberal Arts
  Dania V. Francis — Assistant Professor of Economics, College of Liberal Arts

SLIDE 30 — SEED GRANT WINNER
================================================================================

PROJECT: Living in an Environmental Justice Community and its Impact on
Birth Outcomes

Media stories and policies often focus more on property damage and flood
insurance rates than on displacement or public health. Seed grant winner Lisa
Heelan-Fancher, assistant professor of nursing, College of Nursing and Health
Sciences, is working to fill that gap. Air pollution and climate change have been
associated with preterm birth and low birth weight infants, and Black women have
the highest rate of preterm birth in Massachusetts. Heelan-Fancher's study will
examine the association between living in an environmental justice community in
Boston and Suffolk County and its impact on birth outcomes.

  Lisa Heelan-Fancher — Assistant Professor, College of Nursing and Health Sciences

SLIDE 43 — PORTABLE MEMORIES IN RISING SEAS
================================================================================

This event, cohosted by the Climigration Network and featuring the Fifty-Fifty
art collective's multimedia project Portable Memories in Rising Seas, was an
opportunity to reflect on our relationships to the places we call home in the
context of climate change and sea level rise.

After watching short videos of communities facing relocation due to rising sea
levels, attendees grappled with the implications of climate change as they
manifest on individual and collective levels through drawings and group discussion.
The Fifty-Fifty art collective encouraged participants to explore questions such as:
  - How are you affected by the climate crisis?
  - How is your relationship to the place you call home changing as the planet warms?
  - How can we transform our grief about climate change into action?

Following the event, participants' drawings were turned into prints that become an
archive of our memories and concerns. These may help us to remember our
communities before the hurricanes, fires and rising seas in the future.

LORENA ESTRADA-MARTINEZ RECEIVED $800K EPA GRANT

The US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) awarded Lorena Estrada-Martinez,
assistant professor of environment and public health in the School for the
Environment, a three-year grant of $800,000 to study health risks in Vieques,
Puerto Rico. As a result of contamination created by decades of U.S. military
occupation (including the use of chemical and biological weapons), the ecological
and human populations of Vieques have been significantly impacted.

Her study, "Community Driven Assessment of Environmental Health Risks in Vieques,
Puerto Rico," will be supported by a partnership between her research team and the
local community so that all are equally involved in decision-making and
knowledge-sharing. Her team includes SSL faculty members Bob Chen, interim dean
of the SFE and professor of organic geochemistry; Rosalyn Negron, associate
professor of anthropology in the College of Liberal Arts; and Lorna Rivera,
director of the Mauricio Gaston Institute for Latino Community Development and
Public Policy.

---

ROSALYN NEGRON RECEIVED $250K NSF GRANT

The National Science Foundation awarded Rosalyn Negron, associate professor of
anthropology in the College of Liberal Arts, a grant of $253,862 for a project
entitled "Social & Moral Factors in Post-Hurricane Maria Evacuation Decisions:
Implications for Puerto Ricans well-being."

This study will examine factors that shaped Puerto Ricans' evacuation decisions
after Hurricane Maria. While considering economic and health factors, which
typically shape people's evacuation decisions, they also examine factors that have
received less attention in disaster evacuation studies: the role of social ties
(in Puerto Rico and to the PR diaspora). The project team also includes SSL
faculty member Lorena Estrada-Martinez.
